Space Missions Timeline

A chronological journey through humanity's most significant ventures into space.

1957: Sputnik 1

The first artificial satellite to orbit Earth, launched by the Soviet Union, marking the beginning of the Space Age.

1969: Apollo 11

The first manned mission to land on the Moon, with Neil Armstrong becoming the first human to walk on its surface.

1990: Hubble Space Telescope

The launch of the Hubble Space Telescope, which has provided unprecedented clear images of deep space.

2004: Mars Exploration Rovers

Spirit and Opportunity rovers land on Mars to study the planet's geology and search for signs of past water activity.

2021: James Webb Space Telescope

The most powerful space telescope ever built, designed to observe the most distant objects in the universe.

2023: Artemis Program

NASA's program to return humans to the Moon and establish a sustainable presence there, as a stepping stone to Mars.

Fascinating Space Facts

Discover mind-blowing facts about our universe.

The Milky Way is Massive

Our galaxy contains between 100-400 billion stars and at least that many planets. If you tried to count every star at a rate of one per second, it would take you over 12,000 years.

The Sun's Energy

The Sun converts about 600 million tons of hydrogen into helium every second, releasing an enormous amount of energy. This process has been ongoing for about 4.6 billion years.

Time Dilation

Time passes slower where gravity is stronger. This means that time passes slightly faster on the ISS compared to Earth, and slightly slower at sea level compared to at the top of Mount Everest.

Extreme Temperatures

The hottest planet in our solar system is Venus, with an average surface temperature of 462°C (864°F) - hot enough to melt lead. This is due to its thick atmosphere trapping heat.
